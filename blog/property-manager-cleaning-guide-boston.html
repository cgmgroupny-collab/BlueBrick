The Property Manager's Guide to
Apartment Turnover Cleaning in Greater Boston

Checklists, timelines, cost factors, and Massachusetts security deposit law — everything you need to manage tenant turnovers efficiently.

If you manage rental properties in Greater Boston, you already know the rhythm: leases end, tenants move out, and you have a narrow window to clean, repair, and prepare each unit before the next tenant arrives. Get it right, and you fill vacancies faster, reduce deposit disputes, and protect the long-term value of your property. Get it wrong, and you're looking at lost rent, negative reviews, and legal headaches.

This guide covers everything property managers and landlords need to know about apartment turnover cleaning in the Boston metro area — from the unique pressures of the April 1 and September 1 moving cycles to room-by-room checklists, Massachusetts security deposit law, cost factors, and how to coordinate multi-unit batch turnovers. Whether you own a single triple-decker in Somerville or manage 200 units across Brookline and Cambridge, Blue Brick Luxury and Commercial Cleaning built this resource to help you run a tighter operation.

🔄 The Boston Turnover Crunch: April 1 and September 1

Greater Boston has one of the most concentrated lease turnover cycles in the country. Unlike cities where leases expire year-round, Boston's rental market is dominated by two peak dates: September 1 and April 1. The September 1 cycle is by far the largest — driven by the academic calendar at dozens of universities and colleges — but April 1 turnovers are significant as well, particularly in neighborhoods like Allston, Brighton, Cambridge, and Somerville.

What this means for property managers is simple: during the last week of August and the last few days of March, thousands of apartments across the metro area need to be cleaned, inspected, and turned over — all at the same time. Cleaning crews are in extreme demand, and those who wait until the last minute find themselves competing for limited availability.

The property managers who handle turnover season smoothly are the ones who plan early. Here is a realistic timeline:

  • 6-8 weeks before turnover date — Contact your cleaning company and reserve dates. For September 1 turnovers, this means reaching out by early to mid-July. For April 1, reach out in February.
  • 4 weeks before — Confirm move-out dates with outgoing tenants. Communicate your expectation for the condition they should leave the unit in.
  • 1-2 weeks before — Send your cleaning company a unit list with addresses, unit numbers, sizes, and any special instructions (e.g., heavy pet odor, smoker, long-term tenant who never cleaned the oven).
  • Move-out day — Conduct a walkthrough with the outgoing tenant if possible. Document the condition with timestamped photos before any cleaning takes place.
  • 1-3 days after move-out — Professional cleaning. Schedule this as early as possible to leave a buffer for any repairs or touch-ups before the new tenant arrives.
  • Move-in day — Final walkthrough with the new tenant. Document the condition again.

Pro Tip: The biggest mistake we see property managers make is scheduling cleaning for the day before the new tenant moves in. Leave at least a two-day buffer. If the unit needs paint touch-ups, an appliance replacement, or any maintenance work after cleaning, you'll be grateful for the extra time.

✅ ✅ ✅ Room-by-Room Turnover Cleaning Checklist

A thorough turnover clean goes far beyond a surface wipe-down. This is the checklist Blue Brick uses for every apartment turnover in Greater Boston. Use it to set expectations with your cleaning crew — or to evaluate their work after the fact.

🍳 Kitchen

  • Oven interior and exterior — degreased and wiped clean, including racks and broiler pan
  • Refrigerator interior and exterior — all shelves, drawers, and door seals cleaned; top of unit dusted
  • Microwave interior and exterior
  • Dishwasher interior — filter cleaned, door edges and gasket wiped
  • Stovetop and range hood — grease removed from filters, hood surface, and controls
  • Countertops, backsplash, and sink — scrubbed and sanitized; faucet and handles polished
  • All cabinet interiors and exteriors — wiped down, shelf paper removed if soiled
  • Drawers — emptied of debris, wiped inside and out
  • Floor — swept and mopped, including under and behind appliances where accessible
  • Light fixtures, switch plates, and outlet covers — dusted and wiped

🚿 Bathroom(s)

  • Toilet — interior, exterior, base, and behind tank cleaned and sanitized
  • Tub and/or shower — scrubbed, including tile walls, grout lines, fixtures, and drain
  • Vanity and sink — cleaned inside and out, including faucet and mirror
  • Medicine cabinet — wiped inside and out
  • Tile and grout — scrubbed to remove mildew and soap scum buildup
  • Exhaust fan — cover removed and cleaned, fan blades dusted
  • Floor — swept and mopped, including behind toilet and in corners
  • Towel bars, toilet paper holder, and hooks — wiped

🛏️ Bedrooms and Living Areas

  • All baseboards — wiped along the full perimeter of each room
  • Window sills, tracks, and frames — dusted and wiped
  • Closet interiors — shelves wiped, rods dusted, floor vacuumed or mopped
  • Light fixtures and ceiling fan blades — dusted and wiped
  • Switch plates and outlet covers — wiped clean
  • Interior doors — both sides wiped, handles sanitized
  • Carpet — vacuumed thoroughly; if heavily soiled, steam cleaning recommended as a separate service
  • Hard floors — vacuumed and mopped
  • Blinds and window treatments — dusted (deep blind cleaning as an add-on if needed)

Entryway, Hallways, and Common Spaces

  • Front door — both sides wiped, hardware polished
  • Hallway floors and baseboards
  • Coat closet interior — wiped and vacuumed
  • Light fixtures and switches
  • Laundry area (if in-unit) — top and sides of washer/dryer wiped, lint trap cleaned, floor mopped

📋 Security Deposit Cleaning Disputes: What Massachusetts Law Says

Massachusetts has some of the most tenant-protective security deposit laws in the country. As a property manager, understanding these rules is not optional — it is essential for avoiding costly legal disputes.

Under Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 186, Section 15B, landlords can only deduct from a security deposit for the following reasons:

  • Unpaid rent
  • Unpaid increases in real estate taxes that the tenant is obligated to pay
  • Damage to the unit that exceeds normal wear and tear, caused by the tenant or their guests
  • Unpaid water charges (in certain situations)

The critical distinction is between damage and normal wear and tear. A scuffed hardwood floor from years of foot traffic is normal wear and tear. A deep gouge from dragging furniture is damage. A dirty oven is generally not grounds for a deduction — but an oven caked in grease with burned-on food that requires professional cleaning beyond normal maintenance could be.

Here is where professional turnover cleaning protects you:

  • Documentation — A professional cleaning company provides a dated invoice that specifies exactly what work was done. Combined with before-and-after photos, this creates a paper trail that holds up in small claims court.
  • Consistent standards — When every unit is professionally cleaned to the same standard between tenants, you establish a baseline that makes it easier to prove when a departing tenant left the unit in unusually poor condition.
  • Itemized deductions — Massachusetts law requires landlords to provide an itemized list of deductions within 30 days of the tenancy ending. A professional cleaning invoice makes this straightforward.

A word of caution: Massachusetts courts generally don't look favorably on landlords who deduct cleaning costs from deposits when the unit is in reasonable condition. If you routinely deduct for professional cleaning regardless of how the tenant left the apartment, you risk having the entire deposit returned — plus treble damages and attorney's fees. The safest practice is to document the condition thoroughly, clean professionally, and only deduct when the tenant's negligence demonstrably caused the need for cleaning beyond normal maintenance.

📋 Cost Factors: What Affects Turnover Cleaning Pricing

Turnover cleaning pricing in Greater Boston varies based on several factors. Understanding these will help you budget accurately and avoid surprises.

Unit Size

This is the primary cost driver. As a general range for the Boston metro area:

  • Studio — $200 to $350
  • One-bedroom — $300 to $500
  • Two-bedroom — $450 to $750
  • Three-bedroom — $600 to $950

These ranges assume a unit in average condition. Units at the lower end of the range are those left in reasonably clean condition by the outgoing tenant. Units at the higher end are those requiring heavy degreasing, extensive scrubbing, or extra time due to neglect.

Condition of the Unit

A unit vacated by a clean, short-term tenant is very different from one left by a long-term tenant who never deep-cleaned the kitchen. Factors that increase cost include:

  • Heavy grease buildup on kitchen surfaces and appliances
  • Mildew or mold in bathroom tile and grout
  • Pet hair, stains, or odor embedded in carpets
  • Nicotine residue on walls and ceilings
  • Excessive dust or cobwebs from lack of regular cleaning
  • Trash or personal items left behind (disposal may be an additional charge)

Add-On Services

Standard turnover cleaning covers the checklist above. Some units benefit from additional services that are typically priced separately:

  • Interior window cleaning (beyond sills and frames)
  • Carpet steam cleaning
  • Blind deep cleaning or replacement
  • Wall washing (for smoke or grease residue)
  • Baseboard repainting (not cleaning — actual painting)

Turnaround Time

If you need a unit cleaned same-day or within a 24-hour window during peak season, expect a premium. Planning ahead and booking in advance almost always saves money.

Bulk Discounts

Property managers turning over multiple units at once can typically negotiate better per-unit rates. Blue Brick offers batch pricing for multi-unit turnovers — the more units you book, the lower the per-unit cost.

💡 Why Professional Turnover Cleaning Pays for Itself

Some landlords try to save money by cleaning units themselves or hiring the cheapest crew they can find. In our experience working with property managers across Greater Boston, professional turnover cleaning more than pays for itself in three ways:

  • Faster tenant placement — A spotless apartment shows better. Prospective tenants who walk into a gleaming kitchen and a sparkling bathroom are more likely to sign quickly and less likely to negotiate on rent. Every day a unit sits vacant costs you money — in a market where Boston one-bedrooms average well over $2,500 per month, even one or two days of vacancy easily exceeds the cost of a professional clean.
  • Fewer security deposit disputes — As discussed above, professional cleaning with proper documentation creates a clean paper trail. Fewer disputes means less time in small claims court, lower legal costs, and less damage to your reputation on tenant review platforms.
  • Property preservation — Grease that is not cleaned from a range hood becomes a fire hazard. Mildew that is not removed from shower grout eventually causes structural damage. Regular professional cleaning between tenants is preventive maintenance that extends the life of appliances, fixtures, and finishes.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How much does apartment turnover cleaning cost in Boston?

Pricing depends on unit size and condition. Expect $200-$350 for a studio, $300-$500 for a one-bedroom, $450-$750 for a two-bedroom, and $600-$950 for a three-bedroom. Units in poor condition or those requiring heavy degreasing will be at the higher end. Bulk pricing is available for property managers with multiple units.

How far in advance should I schedule turnover cleaning for September 1 in Boston?

Book by early August at the latest. The last week of August is the busiest period for turnover cleaning in the metro area, and most professional cleaning companies are fully committed by mid-August. If you manage multiple units, reach out in July to lock in your dates.

Can professional cleaning help avoid security deposit disputes in Massachusetts?

Yes. Under Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 186, Section 15B, landlords can only deduct for damage beyond normal wear and tear. A professional cleaning invoice with before-and-after photos provides clear documentation that holds up in small claims court and significantly reduces the likelihood of disputes.

How long does apartment turnover cleaning take?

A standard turnover clean takes 2-4 hours for a studio or one-bedroom and 4-6 hours for a two-bedroom. Units in poor condition may take 6-8 hours. For multi-unit turnovers, Blue Brick deploys multiple teams simultaneously to complete an entire building in a single day.

What is included in a professional apartment turnover cleaning?

A full turnover clean covers every room: kitchen appliance interiors and exteriors, countertops, cabinets, bathrooms (toilet, tub, tile, grout, vanity), baseboard wiping, light fixtures, closet interiors, window sills, and complete floor cleaning. Interior windows and carpet steam cleaning are typically available as add-on services.
